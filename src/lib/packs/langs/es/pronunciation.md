# Pronunciation

Spanish spelling is close to a one-to-one map onto sounds. Learn the map once
and you can read any word aloud correctly, including one you have never seen.

## Vowels

Five vowels, one sound each, always the same length — no schwa, no dipping into
a neighbouring sound the way English does.

| Letter | Sound | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a | /a/ | casa |
| e | /e/ | mesa |
| i | /i/ | vino |
| o | /o/ | como |
| u | /u/ | luna |

An English speaker's habit of trailing off into a schwa (*banan-uh*) is the most
audible foreign accent. Keep every vowel crisp to the last one.

## Consonants worth flagging

- **ñ** is /ɲ/ — the "ny" in *canyon*. `año` (year) and `ano` (anus) are not the
  same word; the tilde is not decoration.
- **ll** and **y** are usually the same sound, roughly English "y" in *yes*.
  (In Argentina and Uruguay both are said as /ʃ/, the "sh" in *shoe*.)
- **h** is always silent. `hola` is /ˈola/.
- **j**, and **g** before e/i, are a rasped /x/ — the "ch" in Scottish *loch*.
- **r** taps once between vowels (`pero`); **rr** trills (`perro`). Same
  minimal-pair problem: *but* vs *dog*.
- **b** and **v** are the same sound. Spanish speakers spell them out loud
  (*be de burro*, *ve de vaca*) precisely because listening cannot tell them apart.

## Stress

Three rules, no exceptions:

1. A written accent wins, always: `está`, `música`, `bebé`.
2. No accent, word ends in a vowel, `-n` or `-s` → stress the second-to-last
   syllable: `ca-SA`, `ha-BLAN`, `ca-SAS`.
3. Otherwise → stress the last syllable: `ha-BLAR`, `ciu-DAD`, `es-pa-ÑOL`.

Stress carries meaning across tenses: `hablo` (I speak) vs `habló` (he spoke).
A misplaced stress is heard as a different word, not as an accent.
