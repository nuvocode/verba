# Grammar

## Gender and agreement

Every noun is masculine or feminine, and everything attached to it agrees:
`el coche rojo`, `la casa roja`, `las casas rojas`. `-o` is usually masculine and
`-a` feminine, but the exceptions are common words — `el día`, `el problema`,
`la mano` — so learn each noun with its article, never bare.

## ser vs estar

Both are "to be". The split is the single most persistent beginner error.

- **ser** — identity, origin, material, time, what something *is*:
  `Soy turco. Es de madera. Son las tres.`
- **estar** — state, location, how something *is right now*:
  `Estoy cansado. El libro está en la mesa.`

Some adjectives change meaning with the verb: `es aburrido` (he is boring) vs
`está aburrido` (he is bored). `ser listo` is clever; `estar listo` is ready.

## Dropped subjects

The verb ending already names the person, so the pronoun is normally left out.
`Hablo español`, not `Yo hablo español` — the `yo` is only used for contrast or
emphasis. Overusing subject pronouns is grammatically fine but sounds foreign.

## The past: two of them

- **Pretérito** — a finished, bounded event: `Ayer comí paella.`
- **Imperfecto** — a background, a habit, an ongoing state:
  `De niño comía paella todos los domingos.`

The same sentence takes both, and the choice *is* the meaning. English collapses
them, which is why learners guess. Introduce the contrast around B1, not before.

## Subjunctive

A real mood, not an advanced flourish, but it can wait: at A2 it only needs to be
recognised in fixed phrases (`ojalá`, `que tengas un buen día`). From B1 teach it
where it actually lives — after wishes, doubt, emotion, and impersonal opinion:
`Quiero que vengas. No creo que sea verdad.`
