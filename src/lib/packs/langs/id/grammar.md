# Grammar

## Word order

Indonesian generally follows Subject–Verb–Object (SVO) word order.

- `Saya makan nasi.`
- `Dia membaca buku.`

The order is relatively fixed in simple sentences, although adverbs and time
expressions may appear at the beginning or end of a sentence depending on
emphasis.

## Verbs

Indonesian verbs do not conjugate for person, number, or tense.

- `Saya makan.`
- `Dia makan.`
- `Mereka makan.`

The verb **makan** stays the same regardless of the subject. Time is usually 
expressed through context or time expressions such as `kemarin` (yesterday), 
`sekarang` (now), or `besok` (tomorrow).

## Nouns and plurals

Indonesian nouns do not have grammatical gender.

Plural nouns are often formed through reduplication:

- `buku` → `buku-buku`
- `anak` → `anak-anak`

In everyday speech, the plural is often understood from context without
reduplication.

## Affixes

Prefixes and suffixes play an important role in Indonesian word formation.

Some common prefixes include:

- **me-** — active verbs
- **di-** — passive verbs
- **ber-** — actions or states
- **ter-** — stative forms, superlatives, or unintentional actions
- **pe-** — people, professions, or tools related to an action

Learners do not need to master every affix immediately, but should recognise
that affixes often change a word's meaning and grammatical function.

## Negation

Indonesian uses different negative words depending on what is being negated.

- **tidak** — verbs and adjectives
- **bukan** — nouns and noun phrases
- **belum** — not yet
- **jangan** — negative commands

For example:

- `Saya tidak tahu.`
- `Itu bukan mobil saya.`
- `Saya belum makan.`
- `Jangan pergi!`

Choosing the correct negative word is important, as each has a distinct grammatical function and they are not interchangeable.
