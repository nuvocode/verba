# Pronunciation

Indonesian spelling is highly phonetic. Most words are pronounced almost exactly
as they are written, making Indonesian one of the easier languages to read
aloud once the basic sound system is learned.

## Vowels

Indonesian has five vowel letters. Their pronunciation is generally consistent,
although the letter **e** may represent either /ə/ or /e/, depending on the word.

| Letter | Sound | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a | /a/ | apa |
| e | /ə/ or /e/ | besar, enak |
| i | /i/ | ikan |
| o | /o/ | orang |
| u | /u/ | buku |

Unlike English, vowels are pronounced clearly and are not usually reduced to a
schwa or merged together.

## Consonants worth flagging

- **c** is always pronounced like **ch** in *chair*.
- **g** is always pronounced as a hard **g**, as in *go*. It is never pronounced like the **g** in *giant*.
  in *giant*.
- **j** is pronounced like the **j** in *jam*.
- **ng** represents a single sound, as in English *sing*.
- **ny** represents the same sound as the **ny** in *canyon*.
- **r** is usually rolled or lightly tapped with the tongue.
- **sy** is pronounced like **sh** in *shoe*.
- **kh** is pronounced as a voiceless velar fricative, similar to the **ch** in
  Scottish *loch* or German *Bach*.

## Stress

Indonesian does not have strict lexical stress rules. In most words, speakers
naturally place slightly more emphasis on the second-to-last syllable, although
the meaning of a word usually does not change if the stress shifts.

For learners, it is more important to pronounce every vowel clearly and speak
with a natural rhythm than to worry about perfect stress placement.
